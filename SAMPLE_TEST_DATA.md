## Sample 1: Climate Change Crisis

**Topic/Title:**
```
Climate Change and Global Systems Collapse
```

**Source Content:**
```
Climate change is fundamentally a systems problem. Rising CO2 levels trap heat in the atmosphere, increasing global temperatures. This causes polar ice to melt, raising sea levels and flooding coastal cities. Warmer oceans disrupt currents that regulate climate, leading to more extreme weather. Droughts reduce agricultural output, causing food shortages and migration. Climate refugees destabilize governments, leading to conflict over resources. Wars disrupt supply chains, causing economic collapse. Economic collapse reduces investment in renewable energy, accelerating climate change further.

The key leverage point is energy infrastructure. Transitioning to renewables breaks this feedback loop by reducing CO2 emissions. But fossil fuel industries lobby against this transition. Citizens must demand policy change through voting and activism.
```

**Expected Flow:**
1. Crisis displayed: System describes the interconnected nature of climate change
2. Action options presented (e.g., "Invest in renewables", "Support carbon pricing", "Pressure government")
3. Select an action and defend your reasoning
4. Receive thermal feedback based on depth of analysis

## Sample 2: Pandemic Response Strategy

**Topic/Title:**
```
Managing a Pandemic: Trade-offs Between Health and Economy
```

**Source Content:**
```
When a new virus emerges, societies face a critical choice: prioritize immediate economic stability or invest in containment? Early lockdowns reduce transmission but devastate small businesses and mental health. Delayed response allows the virus to mutate, requiring longer restrictions later. Healthcare systems overflow, causing deaths from preventable conditions. Workers lose jobs, leading to bankruptcy and homelessness.

However, reopening too quickly overwhelms hospitals and kills vulnerable populations. This erodes trust in institutions. Long-term economic damage from a prolonged crisis exceeds short-term lockdown costs.

The leverage point is early, coordinated response. Rapid testing and isolation of cases prevents exponential growth. Transparent communication builds public trust. Economic support for affected workers maintains social cohesion. But this requires government action before the crisis peaks, when public urgency is low.
```

**Expected Flow:**
1. Modal shows pandemic scenario
2. Choose intervention strategy (lockdown, gradual reopening, etc.)
3. Defend your approach
4. Get feedback on whether you understood causal chains

## Sample 3: Supply Chain Disruption

**Topic/Title:**
```
Semiconductor Shortage and Global Supply Chains
```

**Source Content:**
```
A single factory fire in Taiwan disrupts chip production worldwide. Cars can't be manufactured. Hospitals lose medical devices. Data centers can't expand. This causes supply backlogs lasting 18 months.

Why is supply so fragile? Companies optimized for efficiency, not resilience. They eliminated buffer inventory to reduce costs. Production happens in few geographic locations to maximize economies of scale. Transportation networks are specialized and inflexible.

When disruption hits, there's no buffer. Competing demand for limited chips causes bidding wars. Prices spike 10x. This triggers inflation across the economy. Central banks raise interest rates to combat inflation. Higher rates tank the stock market and real estate market. Unemployment spikes as companies cut costs. Recession deepens.

The leverage point is supply chain diversification. Multiple factories in different regions create redundancy. Strategic reserves of critical components prevent cascading failures. Flexible manufacturing networks adapt to disruption. But these require higher costs upfront.
```

**Expected Flow:**
1. See supply chain crisis description
2. Propose solutions (diversify sources, build reserves, etc.)
3. Explain your reasoning
4. Receive evaluation of your systemic thinking
